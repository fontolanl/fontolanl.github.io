About Me

I am a computational neuroscientist interested in how neural interactions give rise to cognitive phenomena, how brain circuits change during learning, and how mental disorders disrupt communication pathways in the brain. Currently, I am a research scientist in the laboratory of Dr. Sandro Romani at Janelia Research Campus - HHMI.

Education:

I earned a Bachelor's and a Master’s degree in Physics from La Sapienza University of Rome, with a focus in dynamical systems, condensed matter physics, statistical mechanics, and biophysics. I became interested in neuroscience thanks to the lectures of Prof. Daniel Amit in artificial neural networks, where I discovered for the first time that brain circuits can be analyzed and explained with the quantitative methods of physics and mathematics. I then won a scholarship to study abroad and worked for a year in the lab of Prof. Stefano Fusi at Columbia University, studying the efficient storage of ultrametrically correlated memory patterns in Hopfield networks, which eventually led to my Master’s thesis work.


I did my PhD under the supervision of Prof. Anne-Lise Giraud at University of Geneva and Prof. Boris Gutkin at the École Normale Supérieure in Paris. I investigated the neural basis of speech processing, both with computational models and by analyzing neurophysiological data from human patients.


During my postdoc years I have been studying the neurobiological mechanisms of decision-making during motor planning. To do so, I built computational models of brain circuits and collaborated with the laboratory of Dr. Karel Svoboda to test the predictions from these models with optogenetic experiments in rodents.
