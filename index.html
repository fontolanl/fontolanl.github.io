About Me

I am a computational neuroscientist interested in how neural interactions give rise to cognitive phenomena, how brain circuits change during learning, and how mental disorders disrupt communication pathways in the brain. Currently, I am a research scientist in the laboratory of Dr. Sandro Romani at Janelia Research Campus - HHMI.

I enjoy reading literature and comic books, playing team sports and I play the guitar with very limited proficiency. I love travelling, knowing new cultures, and mountain hiking. I believe in economic and social equality, and that, unfortunately, there are no simple answers to the complex questions of this world.

Early education and graduate studies:

I was born in Milan but I grew up in Rome. During my high school years at Liceo Classico E.Q. Visconti I discovered the joy of studying history, philosophy, latin and greek literature. Out of curiosity and recklessness I decided to study the mysteries of Nature and ended up studying physics at La Sapienza University of Rome. I ended up earning a Bachelor's and a Master’s degree in physics with a focus in dynamical systems, condensed matter physics, statistical mechanics, and biophysics. I became interested in neuroscience thanks to the lectures in artificial neural networks of Prof. Daniel Amit, where I discovered for the first time that brain circuits can be analyzed and explained with the quantitative methods of physics and mathematics. I then won a scholarship to study abroad and worked for a year in the lab of Prof. Stefano Fusi at Columbia University, studying the efficient storage of ultrametrically correlated memory patterns in Hopfield networks, which eventually led to my Master’s thesis work.

I did my PhD under the supervision of Prof. Anne-Lise Giraud at University of Geneva and Prof. Boris Gutkin at the École Normale Supérieure in Paris. I investigated the neural basis of speech processing, both with computational models and by analyzing neurophysiological data from human patients.

During my postdoc years I have been studying the neurobiological mechanisms of decision-making during motor planning. To do so, I built computational models of brain circuits and collaborated with the laboratory of Dr. Karel Svoboda to test the predictions from these models with optogenetic experiments in rodents.
